A QHHT session is five hours of intimate, vulnerable work. You're not choosing a hair stylist; you're choosing someone you'll spend half a day with, often disclosing things you've never said out loud. The practitioner-fit matters more than almost any other variable.

Here's how I'd evaluate a practitioner if I were the one looking.

## 1. They're certified in the Dolores Cannon lineage

QHHT specifically refers to the modality Dolores Cannon developed over four decades. Practitioners trained in her method went through a structured certification — Levels 1 and 2 are typical. Ask directly: *"Are you a certified QHHT Practitioner trained in the Dolores Cannon lineage?"*

There are adjacent modalities — past-life regression, hypnotherapy, BQH (Beyond Quantum Healing) — that share some technique with QHHT but aren't the same thing. None of them are necessarily bad; they're just different. If you specifically want QHHT, ask for QHHT.

## 2. They've done their own session — as a client

This one is non-negotiable for me. A QHHT practitioner who has never been a client in a QHHT session is like a swimming instructor who has never been in the water.

The work is reciprocal — practitioners need to know what it feels like from the other side of the recliner. Ask: *"Have you experienced QHHT as a client yourself?"* You're looking for a real story, not a yes.

## 3. They're clear about scope of practice

A practitioner who tells you they can cure your cancer, fix your marriage, or replace your psychiatrist is a practitioner you should not see.

QHHT is a complementary modality. It is not therapy, not medical care, not a substitute for either. A practitioner who understands this will tell you so unprompted. They'll say things like *"I'm not a licensed therapist"* and *"please continue working with your doctors."* If your gut says they're overclaiming, trust the gut.

## 4. They have a clear pre-session and aftercare process

Five hours in the Theta state is a serious thing. A good practitioner has thought carefully about what happens before and after:

- Is there a pre-session call, or are you walking in cold?
- Will they send you a prep guide?
- Do they record the session and give you the audio?
- Is there an integration / follow-up call included?
- What's their cancellation policy?

If the answer to most of these is *"we'll see"* or *"I'll figure it out,"* keep looking.

## 5. Testimonials with specifics, not platitudes

"Amazing!" "Life-changing!" "Wow!" — these tell you nothing.

What you want to read is *"I came in with chronic hip pain that hadn't responded to two years of physical therapy. The session surfaced something I had buried about my mother, and the pain has been ~70% gone in the four months since."*

The specificity is the signal. The vagueness is the noise.

## 6. Transparent pricing

The price for a QHHT session in 2026 typically runs $250–$500 depending on the practitioner's experience and location. There is no industry "right" number, but there is a "no-surprises" expectation:

- The total should be stated up front on the website.
- The deposit policy should be clear.
- There should be no upsells in the room. ("You'd really benefit from a follow-up package, $1,200, three sessions" — politely decline and don't return.)

## 7. They make you feel met, not sold

This is the most important test, and the hardest to articulate.

When you read their website, do they sound like a person you'd want to spend five hours with? Or do they sound like a brand?

When you have your pre-session call, do they ask about you — your life, your questions, your hesitations — or do they immediately walk you through their script?

When you ask a question they don't have a confident answer to, do they say *"I don't know, but here's what I'd think about"* — or do they pretend to know everything?

QHHT works because of the relationship in the room. Trust the relationship cue.

## A note on remote sessions

Several of my clients are remote — we work over Zoom. If you're considering remote, here's what to ask:

- Has the practitioner done remote sessions before? (Most have by now.)
- What's their tech setup, and what do you need on your end? (Wired headphones, quiet room, stable internet, reclining chair.)
- What happens if the connection drops? (A good practitioner has a plan.)

Remote QHHT works as well as in-person for the right client. The right client is someone who can create a private, quiet space for the duration without worrying about interruptions.
